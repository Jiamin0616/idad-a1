Comparative Interaction Design Analysis of Two Experimental Web Interfaces: Justine and Limp Body Beat

By Jiamin Zhang

Introduction

This analysis contrasts the interaction design of two creative web interfaces: Justine by Ding Sun, and Limp Body Beat by Adult Swim. The evaluation focuses on four aspects of interaction design: information design, mapping, characterisation, and feedback. While both websites are experimental and heavily sensory-driven, they adopt very different strategies in how they engage users and shape interaction.

Information Design

Justine mbraces abstraction and poetic expression. It doesn’t offer clear instructions or objectives. Instead, it creates a mood-driven, sensory atmosphere—one that feels immersive yet elusive. Through visual distortion, fragmented text, and non-linear navigation, the user is encouraged to wander and interpret freely. Text here functions more as emotional texture than informative content.

Limp Body Beat on the other hand, communicates through immediate sensory feedback. It avoids formal instruction but quickly teaches users that modifying parameters will trigger audiovisual changes. Learning happens through playful interaction rather than reading or guidance.

In summary: Justine builds emotional depth through ambiguity, while Limp Body Beat relies on high-energy, sensory feedback to generate excitement.

Mapping

The interactive logic of Justine is intentionally ambiguous. Clicking or swiping might trigger unexpected changes—such as visual flickers, audio shifts, or even a full reset. This unpredictability is central to the project's narrative rhythm, which mirrors the non-linear, fragmented nature of memory.

In contrast, Limp Body Beatuses a tightly mapped system. Users can directly control visual and audio elements by adjusting URL parameters. The interface behaves like a chaotic soundboard: user inputs are immediately reflected through movement, glitches, and tempo changes.

Justine disrupts user control to evoke reflection and mystery, while Limp Body Beat embraces precise control to encourage playful, hands-on exploration.

Characterisation

In Justine,the user is cast as a passive observer—almost like a ghost drifting through someone else's memory. There is no menu, no goal, and no clear instruction. Instead, you click and scroll through a poetic landscape, experiencing a story more than participating in it.

In Limp Body Beat, the user becomes an active performer—like a DJ or backstage operator. By tweaking parameters, you control the character's movement, music tempo, and visual intensity. Every small change leads to dramatic, often absurd results, turning the experience into a kind of performance.

In a nutshell: Justine turns you into a quiet observer, taking you into a sensual dream. And Limp Body Beat turns you into a controller of play, pushing you to the center of the stage.

Feedback

In Justine,feedback is subtle and ambient. Clicking might result in a flicker, a sound shift, or a slight visual transformation—so understated that you may wonder whether anything happened at all. This is a deliberate choice: it invites the user to slow down and become more attentive to subtle atmospheric changes.

In Limp Body Beat, by contrast, reacts loudly and instantly. Altering just one parameter can cause the character to dance more wildly, the background to distort, or the music to glitch. The feedback is exaggerated, fast-paced, and sometimes comical—encouraging experimentation and rewarding chaos.

Design motivation: Justine encourages calm thinking by suppressing feedback. Limp Body Beat creates the thrill of participation by amplifying feedback.

Conclusion

These two interfaces represent opposite ends of the interaction design spectrum. Justine is slow, contemplative, and poetic—inviting introspection and emotional resonance. Limp Body Beat is fast, chaotic, and absurd—designed for fun, reaction, and exploration. Together, they demonstrate how interaction design not only shapes behavior, but also deeply influences mood, pace, and embodied experience in digital environments.
